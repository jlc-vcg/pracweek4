How computing is used to care for the elderly in Japan

Rosmino, Claudio. “Japan’s Technology Leads the Way in Caring for the Elderly.” Euronews, 29 Oct. 2019, Source


There are a lot of elderly people.

A trend that is observed recently in developed areas of the world is the natural decrease in population, and the increasing aging population.

While people are living longer and longer, this can also be a major challenge to the healthcare system for a country.

Japan, however, has decided to find digital methods to solve this problem.

How does it work?

Japan's recent efforts are on anonymously processed patient data; data such as blood pressure or heartrate are sent to the hospital.

This data can then be uploaded to the servers by the nurse, which allows a greater amount of data which will make medical care effective and precise.

The best part is that the patient does not need to go to the nearest hospital for such data to be collected - this is all done in the morning in their bedrooms.


AI Navigated Wheelchairs

Many elderly people suffer from immobility as their legs aren't quite what they were like years before.

While traditional wheelchairs are useful, it has the critical flaw that it requires another person to push it.

And although electric wheelchairs solve the problem, the slower reaction timing of the elderly tend to lead to accidents with electric wheelchairs.

There is also the risk of the person getting lost while driving around on their wheelchair.

Therefore with automated driving, users of AI-Navigated wheelchairs will arrive to their destination in the most efficient route, and without running anyone over.

How does this help the young people in any way?

Firstly, getting these tasks automated means that less people are needed to care for the elderly.

This means less tax money goes to the elderly and more towards things that require monetary solutions.

Secondly, computer science students like us have more jobs when we graduate.
